Because we just can't shut up.

Posted by Paddy Foran on 9/25/2011

Second Bit took ownership of 2cloud on January 1st, 2011. We took on the project, knowing about its financial woes, and have been trying to make it sustainable ever since. We've tried donations, we've tried ads, we've tried a quota. Just a couple days back, we launched a survey gauging the response for a subscription model. The response has been pretty disheartening. Clearly, we're doing something wrong.

Idea v. Implementation

We sat in our first Executive SummitTM today, which is really just my slightly-ironic way of saying Tino and I gathered in my apartment for a few hours and yelled at each other a bit while abusing a poor whiteboard with some math. Really, what it came down to is this: it's very hard for us to continue with 2cloud. The past has not offered us much hope that the situation will improve, and our situation is kind of a bad one. We were trying to decide whether to make the service subscription only or have a free tier, and we had a difference of opinions. Tino wanted a more conservative route with a low payout we could be pretty confident in; I advocated for the more risky route that would attract more users and therefore pay out more, but which could also mean no payout. We had a very basic disagreement that was rooted in what we wanted from this app. To Tino, the app was dead. He just wanted to break even, and any thing after that was a bonus. I wanted more. I still believe in the value of what the app provides (in my mind), in the vision we're chasing. As a way of justifying it, I pointed at the statistics. The Android app has been downloaded over 50,000 times and still sits on over 18,000 devices. Only 3,500 people are actually using it, however. That means 50,000 people believe in the idea, have a need for this service, but 32,000 of them didn't have their need sufficiently filled by our offering. 14,500 didn't have their need sufficiently filled, but saw promise in our offering (or were just too lazy to uninstall it). I argued that these numbers show we're right about the market and it's not time to give up yet.

Then Tino noticed something, and it changed everything. I was focused on the idea while he was focused on the implementation. I believe that the ability to seamlessly transfer things between devices is becoming more and more necessary as we get plagued by devices. As a point of reference, I currently have a desktop machine, three laptops (one on loan to a friend, but its replacement is apparently in the mail), a tablet, and two smartphones (only one of which is in use at any given time). Tino has a laptop, a desktop machine, a tablet, and a smartphone. We are the target audience for our service, but if we're being totally honest, we wouldn't pay the monthly fee. Clearly, we're doing something horribly wrong. And that was Tino's point: our implementation is horribly, horribly flawed. It has failed. So we're focusing on a new implementation of the same idea.

App v. Service

A lot of users won't pay us. We know that. Up until now, we've attributed it to a sense of entitlement from the users. We thought "Oh, they just don't understand that there are actual costs to running this, they think we're just making a cash grab." And somehow, we decided this was a problem with them, not with us. We thought that the app was just doing its job so well, the server was invisible. Which is what we want, and making the server visible again was impossible without hurting the experience. But what if we got that wrong? We were thinking about it from our perspective, not the users'. We were thinking about it as a service, but we were presenting it as an app.

I think most people think of 2cloud as an app. They don't think of it as a service, something that costs money to maintain. There is quite a bit of anecdotal evidence for this. Most of our coverage has been in relation to Android. Most of them identify us as an app. Even after we rebranded in March, we're still referred to as "android2cloud". Even worse, when we ask users to pay, there's outrage. They already have the app, why should they pay for it now?

That's really the problem with apps. They're perceived as things you buy once, then own. A service, like Netflix, you pay a recurring fee for. In order to be sustainable, we need to be a service. Once we hit on this distinction, we really got the ball rolling. We started listing examples of services we use and apps we have. We started discerning what makes a service a service, and why we didn't fit that criteria yet. I'll share some of our findings with you:

  • A service is a mutual contract based around a relationship. The understanding is that the customer will pay the service provider X amount of dollars every month and the service provider will render Y service for that month. An app, on the other hand, is a transaction. The understanding is that the customer will pay the developer X amount fo dollars once, and the developer will give them Y app, and the customer will never have to talk to the developer again.
  • A service is introduced in an unlimited market. Consider the internet: you buy physical goods, digital goods, apps, and services on it. All sorts of transactions. You have no real predispositions about your transaction, besides the basic rule that you will pay some amount and receive something in return. An App Store (or Marketplace) has a decidedly different feel; only apps are sold there. There is an expectation that you will pay once for an app, then own it.
  • A service comes from a company. A company is accountable. There is only one Second Bit LLC. There are, however, several Paddy Forans. How do you know which one to sue? How do you find a customer support hotline to complain to? One is a legitimate, established place of business, the other is some dude writing software in his parents' basement in his free time. Which would you feel more comfortable engaging in a long-term contract?
  • A service is uncontained. You can access it from any compatible device by just logging in. An app, however, exists in the silo of your phone. Your data may not transfer, you may even have to pay to download it again, depending. A service is a contract with you. An app is a contract with your device.
  • You use a service. You own an app. There is an inherent sense of ownership in downloading an app; because it's on your device, in your pocket, you think it is yours. A service, however, is being used at the pleasure of both parties. Either of you can discontinue that relationship at will.

Those aren't all the distinctions we came across, but they're the major ones. We think the consumer psychology inherent in apps is one of the many reasons for why we've failed so badly thus far. But more on that later.

A Serviceable Solution

I had to get a pun in there somewhere. What are we doing about this? Clearly, we need to rehabilitate our image to set ourselves up as a service instead of as an app. We're doing a few things to try and get that across.

  • We're going to focus a lot more on the web end. We're going to have an actual web app that we hope will be useful. Again, our idea is to make sharing content between devices as painless and seamless as possible. This means between computers as well as between computers and mobile devices. We're going to add some new features into the mix as well that will hopefully make the web interface even more useful.
  • Our web service will launch in beta. It will launch with a very explicit message that the service will not be free forever, but it is during the beta period as we test the software and gain insight on usage. After the beta ends, we'll be using a payment processor to do recurring payments that automatically bill users monthly.
  • We're going to make it clear that the product comes from Second Bit. We'll be working a bit on branding and making a few small tweaks here and there to make this clearer. We're also hoping to introduce a EULA or Terms of Service, along with a more formal help process. Finally, we're going to make ourselves accountable: if you receive unacceptable service, we will take it upon ourselves to make that up to you or refund your money.
  • One word: Firefox

In short, we're stepping away from Android for a bit to give our web presence some love. It's not that we don't love Android, it's not that we plan on staying away from it or not improving our client; we just need to make it clear that our client is just that—a client, not the whole product. To do that, we need to improve the overall product. I noticed something disturbing today during the discussions I was having with Tino: neither of us could come up with a single change made since January that was an improvement for the users. All we've done is try different ways of making the app support itself. This is a failing on our part; we should have been building a better app, too. I'm sorry about that. We're going to do that starting now. But we're going to do that in a way that helps our sustainability, too.

How We Got Where We Are

How did we arrive in this unsavoury position? Well, most of it is inexperience. I've never run a business or a service before, so I made a lot of mistakes early on. My technology wasn't good enough for the usage it was getting, and that cost us a lot of money it didn't need to. But there are other factors, too. The popularity of Android, for example; most publications that cover us refer to us as an app because they are focused on the Android aspect and miss the forest for the trees. Part of it is the consumer psychology behind apps, the pay-once-own-forever thinking we're trying to work around now. Part of it is a sense of entitlement on the part of our users: I've offered the service for free for so long and been so grateful for their support for so long that suddenly demanding money is quite a jarring shift. Part of it is simply poor communication (ironic that an app based around communicating between devices should have communication problems). We assumed that everyone who cared would subscribe to the mailing list or blog. We tried too hard to not annoy people and to only talk to them if they gave us permission, and people missed out on important information because of this—not what we wanted.

It has been a learning experience since day one. We know much more now than we did then. There's plenty more for us to learn. But I think we're headed in a good direction, and hopefully the users will come with us this time. We're running out of options.
